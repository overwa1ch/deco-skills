# Storyboard Design Guide

Use this guide to create or revise storyboard directions, shot sequences, and boards from the materials the user supplies.

## Establish story authority

- Treat explicit facts, actions, spatial relationships, and constraints in the supplied materials as authoritative.
- Separate confirmed facts from interpretation. Mark an inference when it affects shot order, staging, or the ending state.
- Resolve harmless gaps through economical visual assumptions. Surface conflicts that would produce materially different boards.
- Preserve the requested story boundary. Do not add future beats, explanations, or decorative action that changes the event.

## Choose the useful resolution

Select the design depth from the user's objective rather than from workflow labels:

- **Direction pass:** test visual coverage, rhythm, and expression. Offer distinct alternatives when comparison helps the decision.
- **Locked sequence:** define stable shot order, shot size, composition, movement, blocking, and visible action.
- **Bxx delivery:** use the same locked-sequence rigor and retain the user's `Bxx` identifiers.
- **Targeted revision:** preserve sound panels and redesign only the weak transition, missing beat, unclear action, or conflicting camera choice.

Use as many panels as the key process needs. Avoid a fixed panel count.

## Build the action spine

Map the visible event before selecting shots:

1. Entry state: who or what is present, where they are, and what the audience must understand first.
2. Initiating action: the event that changes the state.
3. Development: the necessary process, reaction, counteraction, or obstacle.
4. Decisive turn: the moment that changes meaning, control, direction, or expectation.
5. Ending state: the visible condition that completes the requested scope and leads cleanly onward.

Give important processes enough intermediate states to remain legible. Compress repeated or predictable motion.

## Design the shot sequence

- Give each panel one primary narrative job.
- Establish space before complex movement when the audience cannot infer the layout.
- Use wide shots for geography and group relationships, medium shots for interaction and blocking, close shots for decisive information or reaction, and inserts for hands, props, controls, or evidence.
- Change shot size for a reason: reveal, emphasis, reaction, concealment, reorientation, or rhythm.
- Place reaction shots where a changed understanding matters. Avoid reaction coverage that adds no new state.
- Preserve screen direction, eyelines, entrances, exits, object positions, and the axis of action unless a deliberate reorientation shot makes the change readable.
- Avoid repeated full-room views, ornamental angles, or dense coverage without a story function.

## Specify camera movement

- Write shot directions in this order: `拍摄角度 + 景别（取景框） + 机位 + [按需：焦段 / 光学效果] + 运镜`. Keep angle, visible crop, physical camera position, and movement distinct.
- Default to a slow push-in when no stronger movement is motivated. State the starting shot size and the slightly tighter landing frame.
- Use `固定` only when the user locks it or stillness itself creates the intended comparison, surveillance, freeze, or deadpan effect.
- For a moving camera, write the start, movement verb and path, relation to the subject, and landing frame.
- Use pan or tilt with a visible start anchor and visible end anchor.
- Use push-in or pull-back with a starting shot size and ending shot size.
- Use tracking with camera side, travel direction, subject relation, distance change, and landing.
- Use handheld with movement baseline, intensity, and the final settled frame or cut point.
- Keep character movement and camera movement in separate clauses. Check that their directions preserve the intended action.
- Replace `镜头跟随`、`动态运镜`、`镜头运动`、`推进感` and `轻微晃动` with a complete physical instruction.

## Make action readable

- Show the starting pose or relationship, the decisive change, and the result of every important action.
- Keep the active body part, prop, target, and consequence visible when they carry the beat.
- Let the crop define the description. A close-up names only its focal subject and the minimum visible contact needed for the action.
- Use blocking to prevent key gestures from being hidden by foreground bodies, props, or framing.
- Split simultaneous actions into separate panels when one frame cannot communicate hierarchy.
- Use same-panel continuous action sparingly: show at most two solid-line positions for one subject and connect them with one clear arrow.
- Keep arrows, labels, and notes secondary to the image.

## Write panel notes

- Use concise, observable language. Describe what can be drawn, not internal psychology alone.
- Bound every panel note to what that panel can show. Keep off-frame causes and continuity facts in adjacent panels or review notes.
- Use one primary visual subject per panel. Add another only when their visible contact, obstruction, or result is the panel's job.
- For a close-up, omit unshown clothing, full-body detail, distant props, background objects, reflections, and other scene facts.
- Describe a viewpoint by its visible result. For a selfie frame, write `自拍角度，手臂伸向镜头`; name a phone only when the phone appears.
- Use readable character or role names inside the board. Preserve project identifiers outside the image when traceability matters.
- When applying the canonical storyboard style, use the fixed under-panel form `景别：...｜运镜：...｜人物动作：...`.
- Fold supplied dialogue into the action note only when the spoken moment locates a visual beat. Do not create a separate dialogue design layer.
- Exclude lighting essays, atmosphere prose, final-video choreography, performance micro-timing, narration, voice-over, and audio design unless the user is merely asking that existing material be located visually.

## Run the design check

Before delivery, verify:

1. The sequence covers the requested entry, decisive process or turn, and ending state.
2. Shot order reads without relying on long text.
3. Every important action has a visible cause and result.
4. Shot sizes and camera moves have clear purposes.
5. Space, screen direction, eyelines, and props remain coherent.
6. The board stays within the requested story scope and visual style.
7. The result contains no asset design, performance design, or final prompt assembly.
8. Every written visual detail is visible inside its panel crop; off-frame continuity remains outside the panel description.
9. Every panel note states a complete movement path with start, direction, subject relation, and landing; an undecided move defaults to a slow push-in.
10. `运镜：固定` appears only when the user locks it or stillness has a stated narrative purpose.
